Interview & client Q&A · honest answers, grounded in the build

The questions — and how to answer them well.

Every answer is true to what was actually built, including the trade-offs and the honest gaps. The goal isn't to sound impressive; it's to be right and clear when someone digs in.

A

Technical

Stack, the MLS/IDX design, the automation engine, security, and the agent loop.

Q What's the stack, end to end?▸

Three front-ends on Next.js 15 (App Router), TypeScript, and Tailwind, deployed on Vercel. Data and auth are Supabase Postgres with edge functions. Automation runs on n8n (self-hosted), which holds the Twilio (SMS) and Gmail (email) credentials. Listings come from the MLS Grid OneKey feed. The AI page is Three.js/WebGL with a Claude-powered chat.

Q How do you show real MLS listings if the API can't search?▸

MLS Grid is a replication feed, not a search API — you can only filter on a few system fields like ListingId and StandardStatus, never on county, price, or beds. So I replicate the active listings into a committed snapshot and do all the real filtering (county/price/beds/sort/text) in-app, in memory. A search query never hits MLS on the request path.

Why this, not that: calling MLS per search would blow the rate limit and can't express the filters anyway. A local snapshot makes search instant and keeps the feed usage bounded and compliant.
Q Why fetch photos on demand instead of storing them?▸

Photos are fetched live, per view, from MLS and streamed same-origin through a proxy that CDN-caches each image for ~50 minutes — under the ~1-hour signing window — so MLS is hit roughly once per photo per window regardless of traffic. Nothing is ever stored.

Why this, not that: bulk-storing photos once paused the account's storage tier and broke the site. On-demand keeps storage at zero, respects the media budget, and is the owner's explicit rule. The proxy is also SSRF-safe — the listing id is validated and only ever expands into MLS's own signed URLs.
Q How do you respect the MLS rate limit and media budget?▸

Data replication runs out-of-band, never in the request path. The photo proxy uses a paced FIFO queue (calls spaced under the 2 req/s cap), a 20-minute lookup cache, and a 90-second negative cache so an error can never turn into hammering. The media budget is a trailing-window 429, so every probe delays its own reset — the proxy fails fast to a branded placeholder instead of retrying.

The lesson behind it: this design is scar tissue — the account was throttled twice by naive bulk work early on. The constraints are now designed around, not discovered in production.
Q The automation engine — is it real, and how does a send actually happen?▸

It's real. A trigger (new lead, stage change, tag) enrolls a contact and schedules the plan's steps. A cron tick walks due steps, evaluates conditions, and for a send step dispatches to an n8n webhook that calls Twilio or Gmail. Every step writes an audit row (enrolled/sent/test/skipped/failed).

Safe by default: with no dispatch env configured, the engine logs test runs and delivers nothing. Turning on real sends is one deliberate, owner-gated switch — plus an opt-out gate and a "Reply STOP" footer on every SMS for TCPA compliance.
Q Why route sends through n8n instead of calling Twilio/Gmail from the app?▸

n8n already holds the provider credentials and is a real automation backend with retry and observability. The CRM calls one dispatch webhook rather than embedding provider SDKs and secrets in the app.

Why this, not that: fewer secrets in the web app, one place to watch every outbound message, and a clean seam to add channels (voice, inbound replies) without redeploying the CRM. The trade-off — a shared webhook secret — is documented and hardened before go-live.
Q How is the whole thing secured?▸

A unified Content-Security-Policy and full security headers (HSTS-preload, nosniff, frame-DENY, Referrer- and Permissions-Policy) across the site and the proxied 3D app, verified with zero violations live. The photo proxy is SSRF-safe by construction. Lead intake enforces content-type, caps body size, uses a honeypot, and is rate-limited. The CRM has row-level security on every table. And there are zero secrets in any repo's git history — scanned across all of it.

Q Describe the agent-loop architecture.▸

An orchestrator scopes the work and dispatches parallel sub-agents, one per app, so different stacks progress at once without one context going shallow. Each sub-agent's "done" is never trusted — the orchestrator re-verifies it against the deployed URL, the database, or a live probe before accepting it. A watchdog guards the process with a stop switch and rogue-commit triggers.

The core discipline: one writer per repo, the live CRM loop never touched (only isolated clones), and a "green" test suite that didn't actually run counts as a failure, not a pass.
B

Product

Features, Brivity parity, and what each surface is for.

Q How does the CRM compare to Brivity?▸

Page-level parity is essentially there: people with a 3-column detail view, transactions, reporting, CMA, market reports, lead ponds, and a composer for notes/email/text/calls. The differentiator is the working automation engine. The honest remaining gaps are the "engines" behind some features — live MLS comps in CMA, and inbound lead routing — which have their seams in place.

Q Is the automation engine a real product or a demo?▸

Real. It enrolls contacts, schedules and ticks steps on a cron, branches on conditions, sends SMS and email through n8n, and logs every run. The one thing it deliberately won't do without an explicit switch is deliver real messages — it defaults to a test mode so nothing goes out by accident.

Q What is the AI recruiter page actually for?▸

Two jobs: recruiting agents to the brokerage and showcasing the AI-automation capability as a service. It's a memorable 3D journey with a live assistant that answers questions and a "Work with me" form — and both the chat and the form feed the same CRM lead pipeline as the website.

C

Business

Value, cost, what it replaces, and honest status.

Q What does this replace, and what's the value?▸

It stands in for a stack a real-estate team usually rents separately: a CRM subscription, an IDX website vendor, a marketing-automation tool, and custom web/3D development — owned and operated by one person who understands all of it. The value is control (own the data and the pipeline), integration (one lead destination), and speed (an AI front door that never sleeps).

Q What did it cost to build?▸

Primarily time plus AI orchestration — no team salaries. The recurring cost is modest hosting and per-use provider fees (Vercel, Supabase, n8n on a VPS, MLS feed, Twilio/Gmail usage). The honest headline is that an AI agent loop let one operator cover the ground of a small team, with the operator's judgment as the quality gate.

Q Could this work for a business that isn't real estate?▸

Yes. Real estate is the proof point, not the ceiling. The reusable parts — a CRM with a trigger→action automation engine, an AI chat/voice front door, and lead capture wired into one pipeline — apply to any sales-driven business. The IDX-specific piece is the only part that's real-estate-only.

D

The skeptical questions

answer these honestly and you've earned the room

Q Isn't this just AI-generated code?▸

AI wrote a lot of it — that's the point, and it's a skill, not a shortcut. The value I add is the architecture, the constraints, and the verification: deciding replication-vs-search, designing the budget-safe proxy, catching a sub-agent that claimed "done" when the deployed endpoint was still serving fixtures, and refusing to accept a test suite that silently didn't run. The moat is the process and the judgment, not the typing.

Q Is it actually in production?▸

The systems are live and verified on their deployment URLs — real MLS data, real photos on demand, the CRM and automation engine, the AI journey with live chat. It is deliberately private and non-indexed until launch. Going public is a short, owner-gated checklist: flip the flag, cut DNS, swap the MLS production key, and turn on real sends. I keep that line clear on purpose — "deployed and verified" is not the same as "publicly launched," and I don't blur it.

Q What are the weak spots — what would you do next?▸

Honestly: the snapshot refresh is manual today and needs a durable auto-refresh store; CMA comps still run on fixture data pending the live MLS wiring; the automation engine's inbound-STOP path and A2P registration are owner steps before real SMS at scale; and the production database of record needs to be pinned before go-live. None are hidden — they're documented, and each has a designed path forward.

Why say this at all: naming the gaps precisely is the strongest signal that the rest is real. A build with no acknowledged edges is a build nobody looked at hard.

Answer to the person in front of you — an employer wants the reasoning and the trade-offs; a client wants the outcome and the value. Same truth, different emphasis.
